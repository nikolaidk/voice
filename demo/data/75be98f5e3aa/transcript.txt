Moore's Law: The Observation That Shaped the Digital Age

[00:00] Narrator: Today I'd like to walk you through the Wikipedia article on Moore's law — one of the most influential observations in modern technology.
[00:07] Narrator: At its heart, Moore's law is simple: the number of transistors packed onto an integrated circuit roughly doubles every two years, with little increase in cost. Despite the name, it isn't a law of nature. It's an empirical observation — a pattern noticed and projected forward.
[00:24] Narrator: The pattern is named after Gordon Moore, co-founder of Fairchild Semiconductor and later Intel. In 1965, writing for Electronics magazine, Moore noticed that the number of components on a chip had been doubling every year, and he predicted this would continue for at least another decade. He later admitted this was, in his words, "a wild extrapolation." In 1975, he revised the forecast to a doubling every two years — a compound annual growth rate of about forty-one percent. Remarkably, that revised prediction held for decades. It was Caltech professor Carver Mead who popularized the term "Moore's law."
[01:02] Narrator: A related idea often gets tangled up with it. Moore's colleague, David House, suggested chip performance would double every eighteen months — which is why the eighteen-month figure is so often misquoted. That performance gain was tied to something called Dennard scaling, the principle that as transistors shrink, their power density stays constant. Dennard scaling, however, broke down in the mid-2000s.
[01:27] Narrator: Over the years, Moore's law became more than an observation — it became a goal, a self-fulfilling prophecy that guided the entire semiconductor industry's research and long-term planning. It drove down the cost of computing, expanded memory, improved sensors and cameras, and helped fuel decades of productivity and economic growth. One study noted U.S. productivity surged to three-point-four percent annually between 1997 and 2004, largely thanks to cheaper, more capable semiconductors.
[01:56] Narrator: Sustaining this progress took extraordinary ingenuity — the integrated circuit itself, CMOS technology, dynamic memory, chemically amplified photoresists, excimer laser lithography, copper interconnects, and, more recently, FinFET and gate-all-around transistor designs. Manufacturers like TSMC and Samsung have pushed to ever-smaller process nodes — seven, five, three, and even two nanometers. In 2021, IBM announced a two-nanometer chip with features smaller than human DNA.
[02:26] Narrator: But there's a catch, captured in what's called Moore's second law, or Rock's law: as chips get cheaper for consumers, the cost of building the factories to make them rises exponentially. The tools alone double in cost every four years.
[02:40] Narrator: So, is Moore's law dead? Experts disagree. Microprocessor architects say advancement has slowed industry-wide since around 2010. Intel's leaders have acknowledged the cadence is now closer to three years than two. In 2022, Nvidia's CEO Jensen Huang declared Moore's law dead — while Intel's Pat Gelsinger insisted it lives on. Gordon Moore himself said no exponential lasts forever, predicting a fundamental atomic limit within a decade or two.
[03:11] Narrator: The article also notes many spin-off observations — Kryder's law for hard drives, Swanson's law for solar panels, Haitz's law for LEDs, even Wirth's law, the wry principle that software bloats to consume the very gains hardware provides.
[03:24] Narrator: The takeaway is this: Moore's law was never a guarantee, but a bold prediction that became a driving force of the digital age. It is now slowing under the weight of physics and economics — yet its legacy, and the hunt for what comes next, continues to shape our technological future.
